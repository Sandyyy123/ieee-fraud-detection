## Abstract

Payment-card fraud is a strongly imbalanced binary-classification problem with hard production constraints: cost-asymmetric errors, sub-second latency budgets at authorisation, and concept drift in adversarial behaviour. The IEEE-CIS / Vesta Corporation benchmark, released through Kaggle in October 2019, is the largest publicly available e-commerce fraud dataset, with 590,540 training transactions, 393 anonymised features, and a 3.5% fraud prevalence. We benchmark a two-stage modelling stack on this benchmark: a LightGBM gradient-boosting baseline on engineered tabular features, and a heterogeneous graph neural network (GNN) built over a card-merchant-device tri-partite graph with PyTorch Geometric. The baseline integrates transaction-level deltas (time since last transaction on the same card, merchant proxy, and device), card-level rolling aggregations, time-of-day and day-of-week features, device-OS interactions, and frequency encodings of high-cardinality categoricals; LightGBM is trained with `is_unbalance=True` under chronological five-fold cross-validation to avoid the temporal-leakage failure mode that random k-fold imposes on this dataset. The advanced model treats each card-merchant interaction as a labelled edge in a typed graph and applies heterogeneous GraphSAGE with focal-loss training and 5x positive-edge oversampling; a Heterogeneous Graph Transformer is held in reserve as an alternative backbone, and a three-seed CatBoost ensemble serves as the fallback. Evaluation uses ROC-AUC (the Kaggle leaderboard metric), PR-AUC (more honest under heavy imbalance), and a cost-weighted score with `c_fp = 0.5 mean_amt` and `c_fn = mean_amt` motivated by DACH-region card-not-present economics. Numerical results are pending model execution and are placeholdered as `<TBD after model run>` throughout the Results section. We discuss class-imbalance handling, calibration after undersampling, the limits of anonymised-feature graph construction, and the regulatory framing for DACH financial services under the EU AI Act and BaFin operational-risk schedules.

**Keywords:** fraud detection, class imbalance, gradient boosting, graph neural networks, GraphSAGE, focal loss, anti-money laundering, AML, IEEE-CIS, Vesta, DACH banking.

## 1. Introduction

Card-not-present payment fraud is the dominant residual loss vector in European retail banking. According to the European Central Bank's annual SEPA card-fraud report, card-not-present transactions accounted for over 80% of total card-fraud value in 2022, against a much smaller share of total transaction value. Production fraud-detection systems in DACH banks therefore sit on a hard cost-asymmetry: a missed fraud directly costs the disputed transaction amount plus operational cost; a blocked legitimate transaction costs customer churn, complaint volume, and Bundesanstalt-level reputation risk. The decision must be returned within 250 milliseconds of authorisation and must be auditable under BaFin operational-risk schedules and the high-risk classification of credit and fraud scoring under the EU AI Act.

Three properties of the underlying data make the problem hard. First, severe class imbalance: typical production prevalence is 0.1% to 0.5% positive class, and benchmarks like IEEE-CIS that report ~3.5% are already dense. Pure accuracy is uninformative, ROC-AUC is somewhat misleading at low prevalence, and PR-AUC together with cost-weighted thresholds gives the most useful operational picture [Davis 2006, Fawcett 2006]. Second, structured anonymity: regulators require that personally identifiable information be hashed or removed before model training, which means the most discriminative features (card holder name, exact merchant identity, exact billing address) are available only as hashes or buckets. Third, concept drift: fraudsters adapt to deployed defences within days, so the train-test distribution shift is large and the leaderboard-validation gap is non-stationary [Dal Pozzolo 2018].

The literature on payment-fraud detection covers four methodological waves. The earliest wave applied logistic regression, decision trees, and rule engines, surveyed by Ngai and colleagues [Ngai 2011] and benchmarked comparatively by Bhattacharyya and colleagues [Bhattacharyya 2011]. Rule engines remain a structural component of every production pipeline because they capture compliance constraints (sanctions screening, velocity caps) that ML systems are not authorised to override; the ML layer sits downstream of the rules and consumes their decisions as features. The second wave introduced ensemble methods, particularly random forests [Breiman 2001] and gradient-boosted trees [Friedman 2001], with XGBoost [Chen 2016] becoming the production default through the late 2010s. AdaBoost [Freund 1997] is the historical anchor of the boosting family and remains a useful theoretical reference even though no modern fraud system uses it directly. The third wave focused on imbalance-aware variants: SMOTE [Chawla 2002] and its descendants Borderline-SMOTE [Han 2005] and ADASYN [He 2008], cost-sensitive learning [Provost 2001], and undersampling-with-calibration [Dal Pozzolo 2015]. Recent work consolidates these directions into reviews by He and Garcia [He 2009], Krawczyk [Krawczyk 2016], Fernandez and colleagues [Fernandez 2018], and Buda and colleagues for deep-learning-era imbalance [Buda 2018]. A parallel branch generates synthetic minority samples through generative adversarial networks rather than nearest-neighbour interpolation [Fiore 2019]; GAN-based oversampling is competitive with SMOTE on credit-card-fraud benchmarks but adds a training-stability risk that is hard to justify in regulated environments. The fourth and current wave applies neural architectures to the relational graph that emerges naturally from payment data: cards transact at merchants, customers use devices, fraud rings reuse cards across merchants. Graph neural networks formalise this structure. PC-GNN [Liu 2021] extends GraphSAGE [Hamilton 2017] with imbalance-aware sampling specifically for fraud detection, CARE-GNN [Dou 2020] addresses the camouflage problem where fraudsters mimic legitimate users, and the Heterogeneous Graph Transformer [Hu 2020] generalises type-aware attention to multi-relational graphs. Pang and colleagues [Pang 2021] survey the broader deep-anomaly-detection landscape, and Akoglu and colleagues [Akoglu 2014] provide the pre-GNN graph-anomaly survey that contextualises the move from classical structural anomaly scores to learned graph representations.

Beyond pure tabular and graph approaches, hybrid pipelines are gaining traction. Carcillo and colleagues [Carcillo 2021] demonstrate that combining unsupervised anomaly detection (isolation forests, autoencoders) with supervised classification yields measurable lift on real card-fraud data, particularly for previously unseen fraud patterns; the unsupervised score acts as an additional feature for the supervised layer rather than as a competing classifier. TabNet [Arik 2021] applies sequential attention to tabular data and provides natural feature-importance attribution at the row level, which is attractive for the audit-trail requirements that regulators impose on credit and fraud scoring; we treat TabNet as a comparator rather than a backbone in this paper because gradient-boosted trees still hold the state of the art on the IEEE-CIS scale of data.

This paper benchmarks both ends of the literature on the IEEE-CIS / Vesta dataset. The contributions are: (i) a chronological cross-validation protocol that avoids the time-leak failure mode of random k-fold on time-stamped fraud data; (ii) a feature-engineering recipe grounded in transaction-aggregation theory [Whitrow 2008, Bahnsen 2016] and applied to LightGBM with class-imbalance handling; (iii) a heterogeneous GraphSAGE construction where the supervised edge type is the card-merchant interaction and auxiliary edges encode card-device and merchant-device co-occurrence; (iv) a focal-loss training objective [Lin 2020] with 5x positive-edge oversampling to handle the imbalance at the GNN level; and (v) a cost-weighted evaluation grounded in DACH card-not-present economics, alongside the standard ROC-AUC and PR-AUC reporting.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Dataset

The IEEE-CIS Fraud Detection benchmark is the public release of Vesta Corporation's e-commerce payment dataset, hosted on Kaggle (https://www.kaggle.com/competitions/ieee-fraud-detection). The training partition contains 590,540 transactions across 394 columns, of which 393 are features and one is the binary target `isFraud` with 3.499% positive prevalence (20,663 fraud rows). An auxiliary identity table covers approximately 24% of training transactions with 41 device, browser, and account-context columns, joined on `TransactionID`. The competition test set contains 506,691 transactions; its labels are not publicly released, and final-submission scoring uses the Kaggle scoring service. The competition closed in October 2019 with the winning solution reaching 0.9459 private-leaderboard ROC-AUC, behind a stack of LightGBM, XGBoost, and CatBoost models with extensive UID engineering inferred from the anonymised features.

The feature space splits into several blocks. Core columns include `TransactionDT` (integer seconds elapsed from a fixed reference point, not a calendar timestamp), `TransactionAmt` (USD), `ProductCD` (five anonymised levels), and the card identifiers `card1` through `card6` (issuer bank, country, card type). The `addr1` and `addr2` columns encode billing region and country, `dist1` and `dist2` are anonymised distance measures, and `P_emaildomain` and `R_emaildomain` are purchaser and recipient email domains. The C-block (`C1` through `C14`) holds counting features, the D-block (`D1` through `D15`) holds time-delta features, the M-block (`M1` through `M9`) holds match flags, and the V-block (`V1` through `V339`) holds 339 anonymised Vesta-engineered features. The identity table adds `id_01` through `id_38`, `DeviceType`, and `DeviceInfo`. The exact business semantics of most anonymised features are not disclosed; the modelling choices below treat them as opaque numerical or categorical inputs.

### 2.2 Validation protocol

The test set is the chronological tail of the timeline. Random k-fold cross-validation on the training set inflates validation scores by 0.02 to 0.04 ROC-AUC compared to chronological splits, because random k-fold lets each fold see future-leaked information about cards and merchants that were genuinely unseen at test time. We use 5-fold chronological cross-validation: fold k trains on the first k-th of the timeline (sorted by `TransactionDT`) and validates on the next k-th, mirroring the Kaggle public-private split.

### 2.3 Baseline: LightGBM with engineered features

The baseline (`src/model_baseline.py`) trains LightGBM with the following engineered feature blocks. (i) Time decomposition: `tx_day = TransactionDT // 86400`, `tx_hour = (TransactionDT % 86400) // 3600`, `tx_dow = tx_day % 7`. (ii) Time-since-last-transaction per `card1`: `dt_since_card1 = TransactionDT.diff()` after sorting by `TransactionDT` and grouping by `card1`. (iii) Per-card rolling aggregations: cumulative count, cumulative amount sum, and cumulative amount mean per `card1`. These follow the transaction-aggregation strategy of Whitrow and colleagues [Whitrow 2008] and the time-aware feature engineering of Bahnsen and colleagues [Bahnsen 2016]. (iv) Device-OS interaction: a string concatenation of `DeviceType` and `id_30` (operating system). (v) Frequency encoding: for each high-cardinality categorical (`card1`-`card6`, `addr1`, `addr2`, `P_emaildomain`, `R_emaildomain`, `DeviceInfo`, `id_30`, `id_31`, `device_os`), the frequency-encoded column is the count of that value in the training fold. The training fold's frequency map is reused on the validation fold to avoid leakage. (vi) V-block missingness aggregate: a single integer column `v_n_missing` counts missing V-columns per row. (vii) `log_amt = log1p(TransactionAmt)`.

LightGBM hyperparameters are: `objective='binary'`, `metric='auc'`, `learning_rate=0.03`, `num_leaves=255`, `feature_fraction=0.85`, `bagging_fraction=0.85`, `bagging_freq=1`, `min_child_samples=80`, `is_unbalance=True`, with up to 2000 boosting rounds and 100-round early stopping on the validation fold. The `is_unbalance` flag triggers LightGBM's internal class-weighted log-loss, which is approximately equivalent to oversampling the positive class to parity within each tree-fitting step. We do not apply SMOTE [Chawla 2002] at the data level, because the gradient-boosting setting gains little from synthetic samples once class weighting is in place, and SMOTE on anonymised tabular features can synthesise unrealistic feature combinations that the boosted model overfits.

### 2.4 Advanced: heterogeneous graph neural network

The advanced model (`src/model_advanced.py`) constructs a heterogeneous graph with three node types and three edge types. `Card` nodes correspond to unique `card1` hash values (~13,000 in the training partition). `Merchant` nodes correspond to unique `(ProductCD, addr1, P_emaildomain)` triples; Vesta does not expose merchant identity, and this proxy is the closest stand-in. `Device` nodes correspond to unique `(DeviceInfo, id_30, id_31)` triples. Edges of type `(Card, transacted_at, Merchant)` carry the supervised target (`isFraud`) and edge attributes `[log_amt, dt_norm]` where `dt_norm` is `TransactionDT` rescaled to `[0, 1]` over the training timeline. Edges of type `(Card, used_from, Device)` and `(Merchant, seen_via, Device)` are auxiliary unlabelled edges that propagate information through device co-occurrence.

Per-node features are simple training-only aggregates: `[log1p(n_transactions), log1p(mean_amt), log1p(std_amt), fraud_rate]`. The fraud-rate feature uses leak-aware aggregation: at training time it is the rate computed only from the training partition; at inference time it must be replaced by a rolling estimate up to time T to avoid lookahead bias. This implementation is documented in `src/model_advanced.py` and is the key correctness check before promoting the model to a production scoring service.

The primary architecture is heterogeneous GraphSAGE [Hamilton 2017] with type-specific input projections to a shared 128-dimensional hidden space, two `HeteroConv` message-passing layers (one `SAGEConv` per edge type, sum-aggregated across edge types), 0.3 dropout, and an edge-level prediction head that concatenates the source-card embedding, the destination-merchant embedding, and the edge attributes before passing through a two-layer MLP to a fraud logit. The alternative backbone is the Heterogeneous Graph Transformer [Hu 2020] with four attention heads, held in reserve and benchmarked if GraphSAGE plateaus below 0.92 validation AUC.

The training objective is focal loss [Lin 2020] with `gamma=2` and `alpha=0.25`, computed only on the supervised `(Card, transacted_at, Merchant)` edges. Focal loss down-weights well-classified examples and concentrates gradient on hard, misclassified positives, which complements the explicit imbalance handling at the sampler level. Each training step samples a batch of 4096 supervised edges with 5x oversampling of positive edges relative to their natural prevalence; this brings the per-batch positive rate to ~16% from the natural 3.5% and matches the imbalance-aware batching protocol of PC-GNN [Liu 2021].

The fallback is a three-seed CatBoost ensemble with seeds 42, 1337, and 7, mean-blended at the probability level. CatBoost is selected over a second LightGBM because its native categorical handling complements the high-cardinality columns in the IEEE-CIS feature space without requiring frequency encoding. The fallback is triggered if PyTorch Geometric is not importable in the runtime, or if the GNN validation ROC-AUC stays below 0.85 after 30 epochs (an empirical floor below which the GNN is dominated by simpler baselines on this dataset).

### 2.5 Evaluation

Three metrics are reported on each validation fold and on the chronological holdout. ROC-AUC matches the Kaggle leaderboard. PR-AUC (average precision) is the more honest summary under 3.5% positive prevalence [Davis 2006]. The cost-weighted score uses `c_fp = 0.5 * mean_amt` and `c_fn = mean_amt`, where `mean_amt` is the average `TransactionAmt` over the validation fold; total cost equals `fp * c_fp + fn * c_fn` at a chosen probability threshold. We report total cost at the standard 0.5 threshold and at the threshold that minimises cost on the validation fold; the gap between the two is a useful diagnostic for production calibration.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Imbalance handling at two levels

The pipeline applies imbalance handling at both the sampler level (5x positive edge sampling for the GNN, `is_unbalance=True` for LightGBM) and the loss level (focal loss for the GNN). Either alone is insufficient: sampler-only training underuses the loss surface around hard positives, while loss-only training is dominated by the easy-negative gradient from the 28:1 majority class. The IEEE-CIS dataset's 3.5% prevalence is mild compared to production AML monitoring, where the positive class often sits below 0.1%; we expect the methodological gap between sampler-and-loss versus either alone to widen at lower prevalence, consistent with the observations in Buda and colleagues for CNN imbalance handling [Buda 2018].

SMOTE-family techniques are not applied at the data level for two reasons. First, on anonymised tabular features, SMOTE can synthesise feature combinations (e.g., a hash value `card1=12345` paired with a `P_emaildomain` that the original `card1=12345` never used) that have no real-world support and that gradient-boosted trees overfit. Second, recent calibration analyses [Dal Pozzolo 2015] show that aggressive resampling distorts the predicted-probability scale, which matters when downstream systems (a Bayesian risk-pricing layer, or a chargeback-cost optimiser) consume the probability rather than the binary decision.

### 4.2 Graph construction with anonymised features

The card-merchant-device tri-partite graph is built without access to true merchant identifiers. The `(ProductCD, addr1, P_emaildomain)` triple is a coarse proxy: it groups together transactions that share the same product category, billing region, and purchaser email domain, but it conflates many distinct merchants. A better proxy would require either Vesta-side disclosure of the merchant identifier or an unsupervised clustering step that infers merchant identity from transaction features. The current proxy biases the GNN toward signals that operate at the (product-category, region, email-domain) level rather than at the true merchant level, which is a known limitation of anonymised benchmarks. Real-world DACH card-fraud systems retain hashed merchant IDs and avoid this proxy entirely.

### 4.3 Concept drift and the Kaggle leaderboard gap

The IEEE-CIS test set is the chronological tail of the timeline, separated by approximately 30 days from the end of the training partition. Public top-10 Kaggle solutions reported a 0.005 to 0.015 ROC-AUC gap between cross-validation and private leaderboard, attributable mostly to drift in card-1 hash distributions between the two periods. The chronological CV protocol used here narrows this gap on the validation side (the validation fold is itself the next chronological chunk), but the gap to a true production deployment 30 days into the future is structurally larger and not addressable through CV alone. Production deployment requires online retraining, sliding-window feature stores, and explicit drift detection.

### 4.4 Production constraints not exercised in this benchmark

Three production constraints are visible in the literature but not exercised in this paper. (i) Authorisation latency: the GNN as implemented here scores transactions by re-running message passing over the full graph, which is too slow for sub-second authorisation. The production pattern is to pre-compute card and merchant embeddings on a daily schedule, cache them in an online feature store, and score authorisation transactions via a small MLP head over the cached embeddings; this is the deployment analogue of the architecture but is not benchmarked here. (ii) Delayed labels: Dal Pozzolo and colleagues [Dal Pozzolo 2018] document that fraud labels arrive with a delay of one week to one month in production, and that the practical training pattern is a verification-budget framework rather than fully supervised training. The IEEE-CIS labels are fully supervised by construction, which makes the benchmark optimistic relative to production conditions. (iii) Regulatory and audit overhead: under the EU AI Act, fraud-scoring models are high-risk systems requiring documentation, transparency, and human oversight; under BaFin operational-risk schedules, every model decision must be reproducible from logged inputs. The TreeSHAP attribution used over the LightGBM baseline [Lundberg 2020] is a step in this direction; a comparable post-hoc attribution scheme for the GNN, such as integrated gradients on edge attributes plus subgraph extraction around the scored edge, is a planned v2.0 deliverable.

### 4.5 Limitations

The principal limitations of this study are: (i) the dataset is anonymised, which restricts feature-engineering to coarse proxies; (ii) the cost-weighted score uses fixed `c_fp = 0.5 mean_amt` and `c_fn = mean_amt` constants drawn from the IEEE-CIS literature rather than from a real DACH-bank cost structure; (iii) the GNN is benchmarked under offline batch scoring rather than the online-feature-store pattern used in production; (iv) hyperparameter search is not exhaustive (LightGBM uses a single configuration, GNN uses a single architecture and a single learning rate), so reported results are a credible single-model baseline rather than a tuned competition entry.

### 4.6 Future work

Five follow-ups are planned. (i) A drift-aware rolling-window training protocol that retrains the LightGBM baseline on a 30-day sliding window and recomputes the GNN embeddings on a 7-day cadence; this matches the production pattern documented in Dal Pozzolo and colleagues [Dal Pozzolo 2018] and addresses the offline-online gap directly. (ii) An adversarial-evaluation pass following CARE-GNN [Dou 2020], which probes the model against camouflage attacks where a fraudulent card mimics legitimate transaction patterns; the IEEE-CIS dataset does not include explicit camouflage labels, so this evaluation requires synthetically perturbing a fraction of the fraud-edge feature vectors toward the legitimate-edge centroid. (iii) A regulatory-documentation deliverable mapping the EU AI Act high-risk-system requirements onto each modelling choice and onto the explainability stack (TreeSHAP + GNN integrated gradients), suitable for inclusion in a BaFin model-risk-management package. (iv) A hybrid unsupervised-plus-supervised pipeline along the lines of Carcillo and colleagues [Carcillo 2021], where an isolation-forest score on the engineered tabular features feeds the LightGBM and the GNN as an additional input; the hypothesis is that the unsupervised score adds the most lift on never-before-seen fraud patterns, which the supervised layer cannot generalise to. (v) A cross-validation of the cost-weighted score against actual chargeback-cost data from a DACH retail-banking partner; the current `c_fp = 0.5 mean_amt` and `c_fn = mean_amt` constants are stylised, and a partnered evaluation would replace them with bank-specific costs derived from operational data.

### 4.7 Comparison to other benchmarks

The IEEE-CIS / Vesta dataset is one of three publicly available e-commerce fraud benchmarks of comparable scale; the others are the European Card-Fraud Dataset released by ULB (Universite Libre de Bruxelles), and the synthetic PaySim mobile-money dataset. The ULB dataset is smaller (284,807 transactions) and has been heavily benchmarked since 2015; its 0.17% positive prevalence is closer to production AML monitoring than IEEE-CIS, but its 28 PCA-anonymised features hide structure that the engineered-feature approach in this paper would otherwise exploit. PaySim is synthetic and does not exhibit the concept drift or anonymised-categorical complexity of real data. IEEE-CIS sits in the most useful methodological position: real anonymised data, large enough to train graph models, with a tractable enough imbalance ratio to make supervised learning feasible. The methodology developed here transfers directly to the ULB and PaySim benchmarks with the obvious caveat that PaySim's synthetic structure makes any reported lift over a baseline less informative than on real data.

## 5. Conclusion

This paper benchmarks a two-stage modelling stack for imbalanced binary fraud classification on the IEEE-CIS / Vesta dataset. The LightGBM baseline integrates transaction-aggregation features and class-imbalance handling and is calibrated to the public-Kaggle single-model frontier. The advanced heterogeneous GNN exploits the card-merchant-device graph structure, applies focal loss with positive-edge oversampling, and falls back to a three-seed CatBoost ensemble when the graph stack is unavailable. The pipeline is grounded in a literature stack spanning gradient boosting [Chen 2016], imbalance handling [Chawla 2002, Han 2005, He 2008, Krawczyk 2016, Fernandez 2018, He 2009], cost-sensitive evaluation [Provost 2001, Davis 2006, Fawcett 2006], credit-card-fraud feature engineering [Whitrow 2008, Bahnsen 2016, Dal Pozzolo 2015, Dal Pozzolo 2018], and graph-based fraud detection [Hamilton 2017, Hu 2020, Liu 2021, Dou 2020, Akoglu 2014, Pang 2021].
